# Running a Community

*Part of our guide to [running an online community you actually own](/articles/how-to-run-an-online-community).*

Running a community is a commitment to people, not just to a place for conversation. Healthy communities make it easier for members to connect, collaborate, and support each other in real life as well as online.

### Community Identity

The best communities know why they exist. Name your purpose for creating an online space in a mission statement, and make your decisions in line with those values. That purpose might be anything from discussing a shared interest to supporting activities your community already does in the real world.

Remember that your digital space is not the community itself. The community is the people you serve. Give members ways to build relationships beyond the group, and don't be afraid to let other activities compete with online discussion. Organizing gatherings, coordinating help, and sharing resources all put your digital space in service of real-world needs.

Communities grow and change over time. Revisit your mission statement, values, and practices now and then, so you can grow without losing your community's core identity.

### Community Leadership

As a community leader, you are responsible for both the spirit and the safety of the space. Your role is to set expectations, maintain trust, and act swiftly when things go wrong. Done well, that work is often invisible, but it is essential.

Healthy does not mean conflict-free. It means disagreements are handled constructively, rules are enforced consistently, and members trust the process even when they don't like the outcome.

Moderation in response to misbehavior is sometimes necessary, but it helps to set the tone up front. You can do this explicitly, by writing a code of conduct, or implicitly, by modeling the behavior you expect from others.

When you write a code of conduct, avoid long legalistic documents that no one reads. Focus instead on your community's values and principles, along with practical details like:

- What kind of language is unacceptable?
- How should members handle disagreements?
- What is off-topic or not allowed?
- How should people share resources or organize real-world activities?
- How can users report other members who are misbehaving?

No matter how watertight your code of conduct is, though, nothing replaces leading by example. The best way to keep your space on-topic, positive, and respectful is to behave that way yourself:

- Use the kind of language you expect from others.
- Handle disagreements with humility and maturity.
- Don't introduce off-topic discussions or disrupt conversations.
- Take part as a regular member, to show how members should act.

Don't underestimate how hard leadership and moderation can be. Many community organizers hit burnout at some point. If the role starts to weigh on you, ask other members to share the load. That can take many forms, from delegating moderation powers to simply recognizing natural leaders and giving them more influence in the group.

Build routines that protect your team's health: rotate duties, set boundaries on availability, and create private channels for support. A burned-out moderation team becomes inconsistent, and inconsistency erodes trust. Caring for your team is part of caring for the community.

### Keeping your Community Safe

Community leaders have a responsibility to put members' safety first. That means moderating harmful behavior and reporting illegal content when required. If your community runs into threats, harassment, scams, or illegal activity, act quickly.

Silence erodes trust. Even when a problem takes time to resolve, make it clear you're aware of it and working on it. Keep a clear record of major incidents, warnings, and actions taken. That record supports consistency and accountability.

When illegal content is involved, follow local laws and report it to the appropriate authorities. Your community's safety depends on you taking these situations seriously, whether you self-host your infrastructure or rent it from a third party.

### The Life of a Community

A good community takes on a life of its own, beyond the purpose or expectations of its organizers. A community is a living thing, and it deserves respect, something to be loved rather than merely managed.

Make it easy for members to contribute in meaningful ways. Offer simple roles such as event organizer, resource curator, or welcoming committee. When people have ownership, they start to bring their own ideas and talents to the culture of the community.

At the same time, don't let a few loud voices dominate the conversation. Make sure new members can contribute without fear of ridicule, and that long-term members keep feeling valued.

When new members arrive, onboard them thoughtfully. Rapid growth can dilute a community's culture and cut it loose from the common purpose that brought everyone together. If growth leads to tension, slow down and reinforce your norms. It is better to grow steadily with a strong culture than to expand quickly and lose what made the community special.

### Bringing It All Together

Digital spaces exist to serve real relationships and the culture those connections create, not to detract from them. If your digital space ever stops serving the people who use it, don't be afraid to close it down, migrate to another platform, or hand off leadership.

Running a community is about people first. A healthy community is grounded in real-world connection, sustained by clear expectations, and strengthened by consistent, fair leadership.
